THE 'BLACK-POTTERY' EXPERIENCE AT LARNAI

Larnai and Tyrshang are two small townlets in Thadlaskein Block of Jaintia Hills, Meghalaya. They would have remained two noncommittal townlets if not for their black complexion crockery which made news and put them on the indigenous complexion crockery chart of the country.

The residents of these two townlets have perfected their craft through hundreds of times of tradition. The two townlets have a combined population of 1987 persons only. Remarkably the ladies outnumber the males (971 males and 1016 females) according to the 2011 tale. Another remarkable thing is that the crockery is made solely by the womenfolk, the moxie being handed over by mothers to daughters.

Every work is hand- blended and shaped without mechanical aids. This way, the potters say, they keep the tradition and maintain the quality.

Traditional Hindu beliefs proscribed the use of crockery for eating, but big matki jars for storing water or other particulars, as well as lights, make up the maturity of traditional Indian crockery. Small, disposable kulhar mugs, as well as oil painting lights, are still extensively used. In India, crockery is still a popular art form.
